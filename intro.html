How Gem Detector Works

Cutting-edge technology that transforms gem exploration through advanced sensor fusion and intelligent algorithms

Water level detection

  • Understanding Resistivity: 
    • Different materials (rock, soil, water) conduct electricity differently.
High resistivity → Dry soil, rock.
Low resistivity → Water-saturated zones.
  • Collecting Data: 
    • Use an electrical resistivity meter to send current underground.
Measure how much resistance the ground offers.
  • Interpreting Resistivity Profiles:
    • Shallow depths with low resistivity → Possible surface water or wet soil.
Deeper low-resistivity layers → Indicate a groundwater table.
  • Estimating Water Depth:
    • Identify the depth at which resistivity drops significantly.
Cross-check with borehole data if available for accuracy.
  • Final Output: 
    • Depth of water is estimated by correlating low-resistivity zones with known geological data.

Gem detection

  • Understanding Magnetic Signatures
    • Certain gemstones (e.g., magnetite, pyrrhotite) have magnetic properties.
Surrounding rocks may also create unique magnetic anomalies.
  • Collecting Magnetic Data
    • Use a magnetometer to measure variations in Earth's magnetic field.
Conduct surveys in a grid pattern to map magnetic intensity.
  • Identifying Anomalies 
    • High magnetic readings → Presence of iron-rich minerals 
(possible gem indicators).
Low magnetic areas → Non-magnetic host rocks.
Sharp variations may indicate faults or mineral deposits where gems form.
  • Determining Gem Location 
    • Compare magnetic anomalies with geological maps.
Correlate with resistivity and ground-penetrating radar (GPR) for accuracy.
Drill test samples in high anomaly zones to confirm gem presence.
